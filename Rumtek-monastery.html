Rumtek Monastery - The Dharma Chakra Centre

Rumtek Monastery, the largest and most significant monastery in Sikkim, is the heart of the Karma Kagyu lineage in exile. Its significance lies in its role as a spiritual and cultural hub, housing rare religious art and texts, and serving as a center for Buddhist study and practice.

History & Significance

The original Rumtek Monastery was founded in the 16th century by the 9th Karmapa, Wangchuk Dorje, but was largely in ruins by the mid-20th century. After fleeing Tibet in 1959, the 16th Karmapa, Rangjung Rigpe Dorje, chose the site to establish his new seat. With the support of the Sikkimese royal family and the Indian government, he rebuilt the monastery, mirroring the design of the Tsurphu Monastery, the Karma Kagyu's traditional seat in Tibet. The monastery was officially inaugurated in 1966.

The monastery is a living symbol of the preservation of Tibetan Buddhism in exile. It houses a community of monks, a monastic college (Karma Shri Nalanda Institute for Higher Buddhist Studies), and a golden stupa containing the relics of the 16th Karmapa.

Festivals, Events, and Rituals

The monastery is a center for various rituals and celebrations that follow the Tibetan Buddhist calendar. These events are often marked by sacred masked dances, known as **Chaam**, which are a major attraction for visitors.

Losar (Tibetan New Year) & Gutor Chaam

Usually in February or March, this is one of the most significant festivals. It's preceded by the Gutor Chaam, a ritual dance performed two days before Losar to symbolize the triumph of good over evil.

Tsechu Chaam

Held on the 10th day of the 5th Tibetan lunar month (around June), this festival honors Guru Padmasambhava and features masked dances depicting his eight manifestations.

Kagyed Chaam

This dance festival, typically held in December, is performed on the 28th and 29th days of the 10th Tibetan lunar month. Monks perform dances to ward off evil and offer prayers for the well-being of all sentient beings.

Cultural Calendar

Experience the spiritual rhythms of Rumtek by planning your visit around these significant dates. (Note: Dates are approximate and based on the Tibetan lunar calendar, so they change each year.)

January - March

Losar & Gutor Chaam

April - June

Saga Dawa & Tsechu Chaam

July - September

Drukpa Teshi & Pang Lhabsol

October - December

Lhabab Duchen & Kagyed Chaam

Plan Your Visit

  • The monastery is open to visitors daily from 6:00 AM to 6:00 PM. A nominal entry fee may apply. Please observe silence and respect the sacred nature of the site. Photography may be restricted in certain areas.

  • How to Reach: Rumtek is about 24 kilometers from Gangtok. Taxis are readily available from the city center.

  • Dress Code: Please dress modestly, covering your shoulders and knees, to show respect for the monastic community.
